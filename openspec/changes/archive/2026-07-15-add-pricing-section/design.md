## Context

The landing page currently ends at Reviews. This adds the last section CLAUDE.md's original landing list still owes: Pricing, behind the navbar's already-dead `#pricing` link — same relationship the last two changes had to their own anchors.

Unlike `features-section` and `reviews-sponsors-faq`, this section is deliberately quiet. Both prior sections earned a bespoke star mechanic (a real constellation, a chained scroll pillar, an ignite-in-place cluster, an infinite marquee). Pricing gets the shared ambient star background and the hero's `ChromaticAberration` filter, but no section-wide star choreography — a rest beat between two motion-heavy sections. The one exception is deliberately contained: the Ultra card, and only the Ultra card, carries a bespoke glow system.

Naming was resolved during exploration: **Lite / Pro / Ultra are pure marketing names**, decoupled from the backend's actual RBAC roles (`USER` / `PREMIUM` / `ADMIN`, already implemented with JWT-embedded role claims and route guards — see `openspec/specs/rbac/spec.md`). Wiring these tiers to real backend gating is out of scope here and would be separate backend-lane work under this project's normal explain-first rules, not something this frontend-exception change touches.

## Goals / Non-Goals

**Goals:**
- Three plan cards — Lite $100, Pro $200, Ultra $400/mo — laid out left-to-right as Lite / Ultra / Pro (visual dominance for the most expensive plan, not price order).
- A star above each card (reusing `StarMark`), sized and brightened by tier.
- Ultra's border carries a thin glow segment that travels the perimeter continuously in one direction, plus an uneven, cloud-textured glow behind the card, both in cosmic purple only.
- The card surface itself stays visually clean — the glow lives behind it; only a thin glass, blurred rim at the card's edge lets the glow show through.
- Buttons get a bespoke 3D elevation treatment: popped forward at rest, pressing in specifically on hover (not on click) — an intentionally unconventional affordance, confirmed with the user.
- A `/contact` link below the cards ("or contact our sales manager for other plans") — a dead route, same precedent `/dashboard` already has.
- Full reduced-motion story: the traveling segment and any button motion stop; nothing about pricing information depends on motion to be legible.

**Non-Goals:**
- Real payment/Stripe wiring, real subscription creation, or any backend role change — this section is presentational, same status Pricing has always had as a dead anchor until now.
- A "recommended" badge or ribbon — Ultra's central position and glow already carry that signal; not asked for, not adding it.
- Extending the bespoke glow treatment to Lite or Pro — explicitly an Ultra-only effect.

## Decisions

**Decision: Ultra's glow system is three layers, back to front — glow, glass ring, opaque card body.**
This is the direct implementation of "the card shouldn't be affected by the glow, but a glass border should show it through":
1. **Glow layer** (`z-index: 0`), absolutely positioned and sized larger than the card: contains the cloud-textured glow shape and the traveling border segment.
2. **Glass ring** (`z-index: 1`), sized to the card's full outer bounds: a thin, translucent, `backdrop-filter: blur(...)` frame. Because it's translucent and blurred rather than opaque, the glow layer behind it bleeds through here specifically — this is the "glass shows the glow behind it" effect.
3. **Card body** (`z-index: 2`), inset within the ring by the ring's own width: fully opaque background, holds all the readable content. Sitting above both other layers and being opaque, it's untouched by the glow directly — exactly "higher z layer, but it got [the glow]" in the sense that the *card* is unaffected while the *section* clearly has one.

**Decision (corrected after real-device review): the traveling segment is a `stroke-dasharray` dash on the card's own SVG outline path, not an element on `offset-path: border-box`.**
The original reasoning was sound on paper — `offset-path` accepts `<coord-box>` keywords like `border-box` that trace an element's outline including its `border-radius`, and that's real, checked, baseline-available behavior. What it doesn't do is bend: an element placed on a motion path stays a *rigid* box that only *rotates* to remain tangent to the path, so at every rounded corner it visibly pivoted like a straight stick rather than curving around it — exactly what real-device review caught. The fix reuses the card's own outline, drawn as an explicit rounded-rect SVG path, and puts three stacked strokes on it (a soft cosmic bloom, a cosmic-light mid layer, a white-hot core) with `pathLength={100}` and a `stroke-dasharray` sized as a true percentage of the perimeter. A dash is drawn *along* the path itself, so it follows every corner exactly, for free — animating `stroke-dashoffset` moves it, one direction, no yoyo, still via GSAP. The lesson: tracing an outline and *moving along* it are different guarantees; `offset-path` gives you the first, not the second, for anything that isn't itself a point.

**Decision (corrected after real-device review): the cloud-textured glow is `feTurbulence` + `feDisplacementMap` with an *animated* `baseFrequency`, and there is no separate static halo layer.**
Two things changed from the original design. First, the original had a standalone glow div behind the card, sized larger than it and filled with a radial gradient — real-device review found this rendered as a hard-edged rectangle, because a radial gradient is clipped by its own bounding box, so the "glow" had a visible straight edge exactly where that box ended. Removed entirely; the travelling stroke's own blurred passes now carry all the light, with nothing separate to leak a box edge. Second, "make the glow line dynamically deforming, like a cloud mask animated randomly" was a real, distinct ask from the original static-seed version: `baseFrequency` on the turbulence filter now animates continuously (`sine.inOut`, yoyo) rather than staying fixed, so the light's edge visibly breathes as it travels. Animating `baseFrequency` rather than `seed` is deliberate — `seed` jumps to a completely unrelated noise field on every change, which pops visibly, while frequency morphs continuously between two states.

**Decision: cosmic purple only, reusing existing tokens.**
`--color-cosmic` / `--color-cosmic-light` — no new color tokens. Explicitly ruled out any other hue for the glow, per direct instruction.

**Decision: `ChromaticAberration` gets `useId()`, not a required prop.**
The component currently hardcodes `FILTER_ID = 'hero-chromatic-aberration'`; wrapping Pricing in it as-is would put two elements with the same `id` on the page. Fix: replace the constant with `const filterId = useId()` inside the component. Every instance gets a unique, collision-free, SSR-safe id automatically — no caller-side prop threading needed at either the hero's or Pricing's usage site, and no risk of a caller forgetting to pass a unique id.

**Decision: card DOM order stays price-ascending (Lite, Pro, Ultra); visual order (Lite, Ultra, Pro) is CSS-only.**
Keyboard and screen-reader navigation should follow the natural, ascending reading order — a sighted mouse user seeing Ultra in the center shouldn't mean a keyboard user tabs through Lite → Ultra → Pro in a different, visually-driven sequence. The CSS `order` property reorders the *visual* layout without touching DOM/tab order, so this is one property, not a structural split. On mobile (single-column stack), `order` is dropped entirely and the natural DOM order — Lite, Pro, Ultra — becomes the stacking order too; there's no "center" concept in one column, and Ultra's glow still does the differentiation work regardless of its position in the stack.

**Decision: plan buttons are a bespoke local component, not a variant on the shared `Button`.**
The hover-presses-in behavior is deliberately unconventional (normally hover lifts, `:active`/click presses) and specific to this section — it shouldn't leak into `Button`'s shared semantics, which every other CTA on the site (Register, Login, "Try COS Code") already relies on behaving the ordinary way. A local `PlanButton` gets the layered-shadow "popped forward" resting state, a gradient fill, and a pure-CSS hover transition that compresses the shadow and nudges the button down slightly — no GSAP, matching how `IntegrationsMarquee`'s wordmark hover already treats a simple two-state hover as a CSS `transition`, not a scroll- or timeline-driven tween. `:active`/click still gets its own, slightly deeper state layered on top of the hover-press, so a real click remains distinguishable from just hovering; `:focus-visible` gets its own explicit ring independent of hover, since keyboard users never trigger `:hover` at all.

**Decision (corrected after real-device review): the button press is a genuine 3D `rotateX` tilt chained with `perspective()`, not `translateY` alone.**
`translateY` on its own reads as a flat 2D slide, not a press into a surface — closer to "the button moved" than "the button tilted back and sank in." Fixed by chaining `perspective(500px) rotateX(...) translateY(...)` into one explicit `transform` value per state, rather than mixing a separate Tailwind `translate-y-*` utility with a `rotateX`-bearing one — both write to the same `transform` property, and Tailwind's own `rotate-x-*`/`translate-z-*` utilities are designed to compose via a parent-level `perspective`/`transform-3d` setup for multi-child 3D scenes, not for a single self-tilting element, so mixing them here risked one silently overwriting the other. `perspective()` as the first function in the chain (rather than Tailwind's separate `perspective-*` utility, which sets a property meant for an ancestor of the rotated element) keeps the whole effect self-contained on the button itself. Verified with a real mouse hover, not a dispatched event (which never sets `:hover` at all): the resting computed style is a genuine `matrix3d`, and hovering changes it to values whose rotation terms decode to exactly the declared `-6deg`.

**Decision (corrected after real-device review): the glass ring uses plain `backdrop-blur-md`, not a `supports-[backdrop-filter]:` guard, and is wider.**
The original `supports-[backdrop-filter]:backdrop-blur-md` was meant as a progressive-enhancement guard, but it silently generated no blur at all — confirmed via `getComputedStyle(...).backdropFilter === "none"` before the fix — so the "frosted glass" effect had never actually been rendering. Replaced with the utility applied unconditionally; every target browser supports it, so the guard was solving a problem this project doesn't have. Widened from a 2px inset to 5px so there's enough of the ring to actually read as glass rather than a hairline.

**Decision (corrected after real-device review): `ChromaticAberration`'s dialled-down `offset` on Pricing is `1.1`, not `0.6`.**
The original value wasn't broken — exaggerating it live in the DOM to `6px` produced clear, unmistakable channel fringing, proving the filter mechanism itself works correctly — it was genuinely imperceptible at `0.6` on this section's smaller UI text, which for a "this doesn't seem to be working" report amounts to the same problem. `1.1` is close enough to the hero's proven-visible `1.4` default to actually read, while still being gentler, per the original intent of dialling it down for smaller type.

**Decision (second round, requested after phone review): Ultra's card is sized up via a wider grid track, not `transform: scale()`.**
Explicitly ruled out by direct instruction, and for good reason: a `scale()` transform would stretch `UltraGlow`'s stroke widths, blur radii, and turbulence displacement uniformly along with the layout, throwing off the careful proportions those were tuned at — either producing a visibly over-thick/over-blurred glow, or requiring `UltraGlow.tsx` itself to be touched to compensate, which was also explicitly ruled out. A real layout change sidesteps both problems: the grid's three tracks went from equal (`1fr 1fr 1fr`) to `1fr 1.2fr 1fr`, plus a `md:p-9` padding bump on the featured card only. Because CSS `order` places items into *visual* track position (not DOM position), the wider track always lands on whichever card is visually centred — currently Ultra — without the grid needing to know which element that is. `UltraGlow.tsx` needed zero changes: its `ResizeObserver` already measures the card's real rendered size and rebuilds its outline path from that, so it picked up the new width automatically. Verified directly — the glow's measured path resolves to the card's new, larger width.

**Decision (second round): the "Ultra" title gets a shimmering gradient and a "MOST POPULAR" label, both gated on `featured` so no other card is touched.**
A `UltraTitle` subcomponent in `PlanCard.tsx` renders the name through `background-clip: text` with an animated gradient fill, plus a small uppercase label beside it. Kept local to the featured card rather than becoming a general card-title treatment, matching this section's established discipline of confining every bespoke effect to Ultra alone.

**Decision (second round, corrected after user review): the gradient shimmer tiles seamlessly and uses lighter cosmic tones, replacing an initial version that visibly snapped on loop.**
The first implementation scrolled a single, non-repeating 220%-wide gradient from `background-position: 0%` to `200%` and then restarted — those are two genuinely different-looking states on a gradient whose start and end colors differ, so the reset was visible, the same class of mistake as the original `offset-path` border segment (tracing an outline and moving seamlessly along it are different guarantees). The fix makes it an actual tiling pattern: both gradient anchor stops set to the *same* color (`--color-cosmic-light`, not the darker `--color-cosmic`), sized via `ResizeObserver` to exactly the text's own measured pixel width, animated with `backgroundPositionX` in pixels rather than percentages — percentage-based `background-position` scales by `(container size − image size) × percent`, which doesn't land on a clean "exactly one tile" offset the way a direct pixel value does. This is the same principle `IntegrationsMarquee` already uses for its seamless loop (two identical copies, shift by exactly one copy's width) applied to `background-position` instead of `translateX`. The lighter-color request and the seamless-loop fix turned out to be the same edit: matching end colors was required for the tiling to work at all, and switching both anchors to the lighter token satisfied "more lighter" at the same time. Verified: `backgroundSize` matches the measured text width bit-for-bit, and the sampled position stays within `[0, tileWidth]` across a full animation cycle with clean wraps, never overshooting into the region that would reveal a seam.

## Risks / Trade-offs

- [Risk] Two SVG filters active in the same region at once — the section-wide `ChromaticAberration` and the Ultra card's own `feTurbulence` glow, the latter now genuinely animated (`baseFrequency`) rather than static, per the "dynamically deforming" correction above. → Mitigation: same scoping discipline `ChromaticAberration` already follows (applied to a subtree, not `:root`); the turbulence animation is a single attribute tween on one filter, not per-frame geometry recomputation across many elements. Still the strongest reason 7.10 (real-device jank check) stays open rather than assumed fine.
- [Risk] `backdrop-filter` has broad but not universal support, and can be costly over a large blurred area. → Mitigation: the glass ring is thin by design (a border-width strip, not the whole card), keeping the blurred area small; wrap in `@supports (backdrop-filter: blur(1px))` with a plain semi-transparent-border fallback for browsers without it.
- [Risk] The traveling segment and button hover-press are both motion that needs a reduced-motion story, or Ultra's card and every CTA on the page lose their differentiation for reduced-motion users. → Mitigation: reduced motion renders Ultra's border as a static, evenly-present soft cosmic-purple ring (no moving dot) — the visual weight stays, the motion doesn't; button hover keeps its shadow-compression (a CSS `transition`, not a JS-driven animation, so `prefers-reduced-motion` governs it via the same discipline the rest of the project already uses for GSAP-driven pieces).
- [Trade-off] Hover-triggered press is a real deviation from standard button affordance (normally hover invites, `:active` confirms). → Accepted deliberately per direct instruction; mitigated by keeping the hover-press subtle and giving `:active`/click its own additional, distinguishable state so a real click still reads as a distinct event from merely hovering.
